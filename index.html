  • Sampling: extract particles from the flow, analyse them immediately or later
  • Due to the sampling, all particle motion information is lost
  • Intrusive techniques are available for all sizes, starting conventionally at 1 nm.
  • While each technique has concentration limits, intrusive techniques can detect very low concentrations and can be used for e.g. clean room monitoring. If the flow is too dense, it can be diluted, meaning there is no real upper limit on the concentration.
  • Equipment is available that applies intrusive techniques in a highly automated way, although care must still be taken with the calibration.
  • Because intrusive techniques are sampling particles, extensive offline analysis is possible, and classical techniques from chemistry can be applied to determine e.g. chemical composition
  • Due to the sampling phase, intrusive techniques can't give real-time data, but always average over a period

So in summary, intrusive techniques are appropriate when information about the particles themselves is mainly what is of interest. The non-intrusive techniques can offer information about the particle motion, but have a less extensive arsenal of particle analysis techniques.

Original aerosol

The original aerosol, arranged from left to right according to size. The subsequent steps may introduce errors in the sizing and miss particles.

Steps in the measurement process

After sampling

Larger particles may not follow the flow into the sampling inlet, and could impact with the surface and stick to it. Sometimes, this effect is exploited to explicitly exclude particles from the sample.

Steps in the measurement process

After internal losses

Internal losses account for particles lost inside the measurement device. These can also stick to the walls, but this loss is inherent to the measurement device that is used.

Steps in the measurement process

After sensor response

Depending on the sensor or analysis method, small particles may not be detected, and the size classification may be wrong id properties such as particle shape or index of refraction don't match the calibration.

Steps in the measurement process

After processing

Processing can involve e.g. an estimation of the size distribution, but the accuracy of this estimate depends on the number of particles that have been sampled. Since there are typically fewer large particles than small particles, the error is usually dependent on the size of the particles itself.

Sampling

Representative sampling

Representative sampling means that the particle properties, both in terms of statistics and individual properties, should not be affected by the trajectory through the inlet system. In practice, this is difficult due to the fact that particles don't follow the flow perfectly, and may stick to the wall. The inlet system may also filter out certain particle sizes more than others, skewing the size distribution that is registered by the instrument.

Causes for loss

  • Aspiration effects
  • Particle deposition
  • Coagulation, condensation, evaporation
  • Inhomogeneities in the aerosol cloud
  • Inhomogeneities in the inlet
  • Clogging of the inlet
  • Particle resuspension

Different effects inside and around the sampling inlet may cause particles to be lost or statistics to be skewed. The effects of these can be quantified using efficiencies.

Efficiencies

Aspiration efficiency

$η_\mathrm{asp} = \frac{c_\mathrm{in}}{c_\mathrm{aerosol}}$

The aspiration efficiency is the ratio of the concentration of particles entering the inlet with respect to the concentration of the particles in the original aerosol. It depends on the particle size (or Stokes number) and the shape of the inlet, its orientation with respect to the flow and the relative velocity between the velocity of the cloud and the flow velocity in the inlet.

Efficiencies

Transmission efficiency

$η_\mathrm{trans} ≡ \frac{c_\mathrm{out}}{c_\mathrm{in}}$

The transmission efficiency is the ratio of the concentration of particles leaving the inlet with respect to the concentration of the particles entering the inlet. It depends on the particle size (or Stokes number) and the shape of the inlet, its orientation with respect to gravity and the Reynolds number of the flow in the inlet.

Isoaxial aspiration efficiency

Rader & Marple, 1987:

$η_\mathrm{asp} = 1+\left(\frac{u_0}{u} - 1\right) \left(1 - \frac{1}{1+3.77 \mathrm{St}^{0.883}}\right)$

  • Maximum error 5 % if:
    • $0.005 < \mathrm{St} < 10$
    • $0.5 < \frac{u_0}{u} < 5$
  • Note: $\mathrm{St} ≡ \frac{τu_0}{d}$

Correlations exist for the aspiration efficiency, such as this one derived by Rader & Marple for isoaxial sampling. The efficiency depends on particle size (Stokes number, here defined with respect to the sampling pipe diameter) and the flow velocity ratio.

Isoaxial aspiration efficiency

$η_\mathrm{asp} = 1+\left(\color{red}{\frac{u_0}{u}} - 1\right) \left(1 - \frac{1}{1+3.77 \color{red}{\mathrm{St}}^{0.883}}\right)$

Here, we can see the effect of both parameters. Note that for sub-isokinetic sampling we may get an aspiration efficiency higher than one, because particles fall through the streamlines into the sampling inlet.

Anisoaxial sampling

If we take into account an angle between the flow and the sampling inlet, the aspiration efficiency will decrease further, mainly due to inertial effects, as can be seen by the fact that the decrease is sharpest for the largest Stokes numbers.

In the case of calm air, particle inertia quickly reduces the aspiration efficiency as the Stokes number increases. It is also possible to combine aspiration efficiencies for calm air with those from moving air using a blending function.

Transmission efficiency

$η_\mathrm{trans} ≡ \frac{c_\mathrm{out}}{c_\mathrm{in}}$

Once past the inlet plane, the particles are still subjected to losses due to gravitational settling, inertia, diffusion and turbophoresis.

Transmission losses

Loss due to inertia

Losses at the inlet happen in the case of sub-isokinetic sampling, where particles that can't completely follow the stream tube hit the wall. In the case of super-isokinetic sampling, the inertial losses can be neglected in a first approximation, or the flow contraction at the inlet with the associated separation bubble can cause inertial deposition.

Transmission losses

Loss due to gravity

Gravitational settling can be avoided by keeping the gas speed large with respect to the settling speed, and by placing the sampling inlet and ducting vertically.

Transmission losses

Loss due to brownian motion

Very small (< 100 nm) particles move due to the molecular motions in the fluid.

Transmission losses

Loss due to turbophoresis

Due to turbophoresis, particles in turbulent flow tend to migrate towards the wall, depending on their Stokes number.

Transmission losses

Loss due to thermophoresis

Thermophoresis effects occur when the gas stream is hotter than the probe walls. It can be avoided by heating the probe or cooling the gas.

Transmission losses

Loss due to electrophoresis

Electrophoresis is avoided by using a conductive material for the pipes. Avoid materials such as Teflon.

Dilution

$DR = \frac{Q_t}{Q_s} = \frac{Q_d + Q_s}{Q_s} = \frac{Q_t}{Q_t-Q_d}$

Due to the conservation of mass, we only need to measure two out of the three mass flows involved in the dilution process. Given that mass flows are measured with a given accuracy, the choice will influence the accuracy of the result.

Measure $Q_t$ and $Q_s$ ($DR = \frac{Q_t}{Q_s}$)

\begin{eqnarray} (Δ DR)^2 &=& \left(\frac{∂ DR}{∂Q_t}\right)^2 (ΔQ_t)^2 + \left(\frac{∂ DR}{∂Q_s}\right)^2 (ΔQ_s)^2\\ &=& \frac{1}{Q_s^2} (ΔQ_t)^2 + \frac{Q_t^2}{Q_s^4} (ΔQ_t)^2\\ &=& \frac{Q_t^2}{Q_s^2} \left(\frac{ΔQ_t}{Q_t}\right)^2 + \frac{Q_t^2}{Q_s^2} \left(\frac{ΔQ_s}{Q_s}\right)^2 \end{eqnarray}

$⇒ Δ_\% DR = \sqrt{\left(\Delta_\% Q_t\right)^2 + \left(\Delta_\% Q_s\right)^2}$

Measure $Q_d$ and $Q_s$ ($DR = \frac{Q_d + Q_s}{Q_s}$)

\begin{eqnarray} (Δ DR)^2 &=& \frac{1}{Q_s^2} (ΔQ_d)^2 + \frac{Q_d^2}{Q_s^4} (ΔQ_s)^2\\ &=& \frac{Q_d^2}{Q_s^2} (Δ_\%Q_d)^2 + \frac{Q_d^2}{Q_s^2} (Δ_\%Q_s)^2\\ &=& (DR-1)^2\left((Δ_\%Q_d)^2 + (Δ_\%Q_s)^2\right)\\ \end{eqnarray}

$⇒ Δ_\% DR = \left(1-\frac{1}{DR}\right)\sqrt{(\Delta_\% Q_d)^2 + (\Delta_\% Q_s)^2}$

Measure $Q_d$ and $Q_t$ ($DR = \frac{Q_t}{Q_t-Q_d}$)

\begin{eqnarray} (Δ DR)^2 &=& \frac{Q_t^2}{(Q_t - Q_d)^4} (ΔQ_d)^2 + \frac{Q_d^2}{(Q_t - Q_d)^4} (ΔQ_t)^2\\ &=& \left(\frac{Q_d}{Q_t-Q_d}DR\right)^2\left((Δ_\%Q_d)^2 + (Δ_\%Q_t)^2\right)\\ &=& ((DR-1)DR)^2\left((Δ_\%Q_d)^2 + (Δ_\%Q_t)^2\right)\\ \end{eqnarray}

$⇒ Δ_\% DR = \left(DR-1\right)\sqrt{(\Delta_\% Q_d)^2 + (\Delta_\% Q_t)^2}$

Dilution ratio error

The best approach is to measure the dilution and sampling flows, although the advantage levels off as the dilution ratio increases.

Concentration limits

  • Upper limit
    • Limited by coincidence and recovery time
    • Limit sample flow rate
    • Dilute inlet
  • Lower limit
    • Depends on number of "noise pulses"
    • Lower limit = 1 order above noise count
    • Might be leaks or recirculation

Size limits

  • Lower limit
    • Detector limit
    • Gradual decrease in the sub-μm range
    • Increase lightsource power
    • Problem: Rayleigh-scattering of air (1 mm³ = 0.2 μm)
    • Not always 50 % efficiency for lower limit!
  • Lower limit
    • Depends on number of "noise pulses"
    • Lower limit = 1 order above noise count
